Q2 2026 total revenue is down 5.3% vs Q2 2025 — a relatively contained revenue decline despite the occupancy gap being much larger (−16 pts average). The critical question: did the higher rate cause the volume drop, or did something else?

06 · Analysis

Key Insights

Critical

Room Only + Taghazout Villa package collapsed −94% — from €45,913 across 82 bookings in 2025, to €2,583 from just 1 booking in 2026. This single package shift accounts for nearly half the total revenue gap. It was the second-largest revenue package in 2025. Was it discontinued, deprioritised, or simply not promoted? This needs to be investigated urgently.

Critical

Summer Surf Package down −28% in revenue and −40% in bookings (67 → 40). This was the top revenue package in 2025 at €81K. The surf package buyer is your highest-value guest. Losing 27 of them in a single quarter has a disproportionate impact on total revenue and the property's positioning as a surf destination.

High

June bookings fell from 220 to 152 (−31%) — the steepest volume drop. Yet revenue only fell 7% because average booking value surged from €575 to €774 (+35%). June 2026 had far fewer guests paying significantly more. This points to a pricing or availability change that filtered out shorter, cheaper stays.

High

Room Only Last Minute revenue jumped +48% (€51,837 → €76,909) with more bookings (121 → 131). The property is increasingly reliant on last-minute discounting to fill rooms. While it protects occupancy, it is replacing higher-value surf packages with lower-margin room-only stays — a structural shift in guest mix that compresses long-term revenue potential.

High

Short stays are disappearing. 1-night bookings fell from 93 to 62 (−33%) and 2-night bookings from 99 to 76 (−23%). This is consistent with a minimum stay policy or pricing structure that pushed out short-stay guests — but those guests represented significant volume in 2025 and their absence is felt in booking counts.

Positive

May 2026 is essentially flat vs 2025 — €126,459 vs €126,250, a difference of just €209. With slightly more bookings (168 vs 165). May is the only month where 2026 holds its ground entirely. The May strategy — whatever pricing and channel mix produced this — should be studied and extended to April and June.

Positive

Surf Experiences revenue up +16% (€53,204 → €61,812) with more bookings (42 → 48). This is a genuine bright spot. Demand for surf experiences is growing year-on-year and these guests have a higher average booking value. This package should anchor the paid media strategy for Q3.

Positive

ADR up +8.6% (€185 → €201) — the rate increase is working. The revenue gap is entirely a volume problem, not a rate problem. The property is charging more per night and guests are paying it. The task for Q3 is replicating May's volume at June's rates.

Note

UK market revenue grew +24% (€88K → €110K) despite fewer overall bookings — UK guests are paying more and staying longer. UK now represents 38% of accommodation revenue vs 29% in 2025. The concentration risk is increasing: a disruption to UK travel demand would be felt acutely.

Note

Marketing channel attribution is empty in both datasets. Without knowing which channel (Meta, Google, organic, direct) drove each booking, calculating true ROAS is impossible. This is a critical gap in the reporting infrastructure that should be addressed in Bookinglayer before the next export.
